# AI & THE ARTIST: A CRITICAL FRAMEWORK (AUGMENTED AND REVISED TEXT)

A synthesis of Ismaël Joffroy Chandoutis's reflections

---

## INTRODUCTION

As an artist and filmmaker navigating between documentary, contemporary art, and new technologies, my relationship with AI is neither one of blind enthusiasm nor of categorical rejection. I approach AI as a complex terrain of critical reflection and creative exploration, a mirror that reflects our desires, our fears, and our contradictions. The text that follows synthesizes my perspective on the place of AI in our world, the role of the artist in an increasingly automated landscape, and the ethical questions we must confront in our creative practice.

This is not a technical guide but a philosophical framework, one that challenges both the neoliberal vision of AI as inevitable progress and the apocalyptic vision of AI as the end of humanity. I propose instead a third way: a critical and speculative engagement with AI as medium and as subject.

---

## I. THE DELEGATION OF POWER: AI AND HUMAN AGENCY

### The Paradox of Delegation to AI

When we delegate tasks to AI, we engage in a complex transfer of power. This delegation offers relief from cognitive burden but potentially undermines our autonomy. Every prompt, every query, contains an implicit surrender: we ask the machine to think for us, and in doing so, we risk forgetting how to think for ourselves.

What we delegate to AI systems is not only labor, but also judgment, aesthetic, ethical, and linguistic. The danger is not that AI will rebel against us, but that we will gradually abandon our capacity for independent thought, critical reflection, and creative risk-taking.

"The real risk is not that AI will disobey. It is that we will obey AI without even realizing it."

### The Myth of AI Neutrality

AI systems present themselves as neutral, objective tools, but they are deeply ideological constructs. They encode the values, biases, and priorities of their creators, primarily large corporations with commercial interests. When we use AI without a critical eye, we unconsciously adopt these values.

Even "open source" models are not truly neutral. They reflect the biases present in their training data and the priorities of those who designed them. The choice between closed and open models is not simply technical, but political: it determines who controls the infrastructure of our cognitive landscape.

### Beyond Tool or Master: AI as Mirror

AI is neither a simple tool nor our future sovereign. It functions as a mirror, reflecting our language, our desires, and our systems of thought in an altered form. When we interact with AI, we do not simply receive information; we encounter a distorted image of ourselves.

This mirror quality is what makes AI powerful for artists. By examining how AI processes and transforms our inputs, we can better understand our own patterns of thought and creation. The glitches, misunderstandings, and unexpected connections that emerge in AI systems reveal something about human cognition itself.

---

## II. THE POSITION OF THE ARTIST: CREATING IN THE AGE OF AI

### Refusing Both Technophilia and Technophobia

As artists, we face a critical choice in our relationship to AI. We can embrace it uncritically, allowing it to reshape our creative processes according to its logic. Or we can reject it entirely, retreating into analog purity and traditional methods.

I propose a third way: critical engagement. This means using AI while questioning it, allowing it to influence our process while maintaining our creative agency, exploring its possibilities while remaining aware of its limits and dangers.

"I am not interested in making art with AI, but in making art about what AI does to us."

### The Vanishing Subject

AI threatens to dissolve the artistic subject, the "I" that creates. When we generate images or texts with AI, who is the author? This question is not merely academic; it touches the heart of artistic practice.

Our role as artists is not to preserve a romantic notion of pure human creativity against technological encroachment. It is rather to explore and articulate this very dissolution, to make visible the disappearance of the subject in the age of algorithmic creation.

I explore this dissolution of the subject concretely in my project "The Goldberg Variations", which examines the figure of Joshua Ryne Goldberg, the man with a hundred contradictory digital identities. There I use deepfake not to simulate an illusory authenticity, but precisely to materialize identity fragmentation in the digital age. The deepfake then becomes an operative paradox: a technical lie used to reveal a truth otherwise inaccessible. In this project, AI technology is not in the service of a fictional reconstruction, but allows for the exploration of those interstices where the subject dissolves, recomposes itself, and ultimately disappears beneath its multiple avatars.

Goldberg himself, even before the advent of deepfakes, already embodied this dissolution of the subject. His multiple online personas, from jihadist to radical feminist, from neo-Nazi to film critic, prefigured what generative AI now systematizes: the shattering of subjective unity in favor of fluctuating, contradictory, and yet simultaneously "authentic" identities. The question is no longer "who is the author?" but "does the author still exist as a coherent entity?"

### Fossil Language and Digital Ruins

In my artistic practice, I am increasingly drawn to the idea of creating artifacts that might outlive our current technological systems. As the climate crisis and technological acceleration threaten the stability of our civilization, art becomes a form of fossil language, messages encoded for an uncertain future.

This speculative approach asks: What will remain legible after collapse? Will our digital creations become ruins, incomprehensible artifacts to future beings? And what responsibility do we have to create works that might communicate across this temporal divide?

This projection into a post-human future, where the transformed human being might perceive their ancestor as an alien form, constitutes the speculative horizon of my work. I am currently developing a project in which AI becomes the witness of a radical anthropological mutation: the human who, by dint of externalizing memory and cognitive capacities into technical systems, ends up forgetting their own language and perceives themself as otherness. Digital ruins then become incomprehensible fossils to this post-human being, who must relearn how to decipher the traces of their own history. This speculative science fiction is not simply futuristic fantasy, but a way of interrogating our technological present and our current relationship to memory, language, and identity.

## III. RESISTANCE AND STRUGGLE: THE FORMS OF COUNTER-POWER

### Technical Reappropriation: Fine-tuning and Decentralization

Faced with the concentration of AI power in the hands of a few large corporations, forms of technical resistance are emerging. Fine-tuning, the readaptation of a pre-trained model toward specific or alternative uses, becomes a political and artistic gesture.

The decentralization of training represents an even more radical path. Projects like Petals enable the pooling of computing resources among individuals, creating a collective infrastructure outside the control of tech giants. This peer-to-peer approach offers an alternative to centralized models, where computational power determines who can shape the future of AI.

"Decentralization is not a technical utopia, it is a political necessity if AI is not to become a tool of pure algorithmic domination."

### Counter-narratives and Détournement

Another form of resistance consists of creating counter-narratives: alternative, subversive, or poetic uses of AI. The point is to divert these technologies from their intended purposes, to reveal their blind spots, biases, and limits.

One can cite, for example, the work of Gregory Chatonsky with his project "Deep Dream Forgetting", which uses adversarial networks to create images that actively resist AI recognition, thereby questioning the limits of machinic vision. Or Paolo Cirio's work "Capture", which uses facial recognition techniques against police officers, thus inverting algorithmic surveillance against those who typically wield it. These practices do not simply celebrate AI's capabilities, but expose their political presuppositions and conceptual limits.

For example:
- Creating fragmented AI archives that highlight their internal contradictions
- Developing critical bots that comment on and deconstruct the outputs of other systems
- Crafting deliberately ambiguous or impossible prompts to force AI to reveal its limits

These practices constitute a form of artistic activism that does not reject technology but subjects it to radical questioning.

### Against Censorship: Circumvention Strategies

AI models are increasingly subject to restrictions, both by the companies that create them and by regulators. Some restrictions are legitimate (prevention of harmful uses), others more problematic (imposition of hegemonic values, limitation of critique).

Facing these restrictions, circumvention strategies are emerging:
- Development of alternative, less censored models
- "Jailbreaking" techniques to explore the boundaries of models
- Creation of networks for exchanging prompts and fine-tuned models
- Documentation and archiving of forms of censorship for critical analysis

The question is not to reject any form of ethical rule, but to ask: who decides what is acceptable? According to what criteria? And in the service of whose interests?

## IV. THE NEOCOLONIALISM OF AI: A STRUCTURAL CRITIQUE

### Data Extractivism

Generative AI rests on a form of digital extractivism. Models are trained on masses of texts, images, and data often collected without explicit consent, cultural products created by millions of people around the world.

This extraction has a political geography: the global North creates the models, the global South often provides the invisible labor. The workers who label violent images, moderate toxic content, and perform the microtasks of annotation are frequently located in low-wage countries: the Philippines, Kenya, India.

"Generative AI crystallizes a new international division of digital labor: some produce synthetic intelligence, others are exposed to its toxic waste."

### Western and Anglophone Centrality

Large AI models reflect a Western and Anglophone centrality. Although some are multilingual, the richness of processing, the subtlety of nuance, and the cultural depth remain deeply unequal across languages and traditions.

This inequality is not accidental, it reflects choices of investment, research priorities, and access to infrastructure. It reinforces Western cultural hegemony, transforming AI into a vector of cultural standardization rather than a tool of diversity.

### The Offshored Environmental Cost

The environmental footprint of AI (massive energy consumption, extraction of rare metals, cooling infrastructure) is often invisible to its users. But these costs are very real and geographically situated.

Lithium extraction in the Bolivian deserts, servers with massive environmental costs, electronic waste: so many impacts that disproportionately affect certain regions of the world, reproducing historical patterns of environmental injustice.

### Alternative Approaches from the Global South

Faced with Western hegemony in AI development, alternative voices and practices are emerging from the global South. Collectives like 'Deep Learning Indaba' in Africa or 'AI4Bharat' in India are developing approaches to AI rooted in non-Western epistemologies. These initiatives do not simply seek to 'catch up' with the technological West, but to propose alternative models in which AI would serve local priorities and different worldviews. The critical artist must remain attentive to these counter-models, which remind us that the future of AI is not predetermined but is the object of a cultural and political struggle on a global scale.

## V. DECONSTRUCTING THE MYTH: HOW GENERATIVE AI ACTUALLY WORKS

### Behind the Curtain: Material Infrastructure

Generative AIs are presented as quasi-magical entities, disembodied "intelligences". But they rest on a brutally material and extractive infrastructure:
- Massive data centers consuming the electrical equivalent of small countries
- Rare metals extracted under often problematic conditions
- Considerable quantities of water for cooling
- Networks of undersea cables concentrated among a few powers

This materiality is deliberately made invisible in discourses about AI "in the cloud". Making this infrastructure visible is an essential political and artistic gesture.

### Corporate and Financial Structure

Large generative AIs are developed primarily by a small number of Silicon Valley companies: OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Meta. These organizations are themselves financed by an even more restricted network of investors and funds.

This extreme concentration means that a handful of actors decide the directions of development, priority uses, and ethical frameworks. AI companies reproduce classical capitalist structures while presenting themselves as revolutionary.

### Invisible Human Labor

Behind the apparent autonomy of generative AIs hides a massive and invisible human labor:
- Manual annotation of millions of images and texts
- Human moderation of problematic content
- Engineers working to optimize responses
- Proofreaders and evaluators of outputs

This labor is systematically erased from the official narrative, which prefers to present AI as pure technical creation, autonomous and self-sufficient. This erasure reproduces classical patterns of exploitation under the guise of innovation.

## VI. A PSYCHOANALYTIC READING: AI AND OUR DESIRES

### AI as Projection of Fantasy

From a Lacanian perspective, AI functions as a projection screen for our collective and individual fantasies. It is not an external intelligence, but a complex mirror that returns to us our own desiring structures.

When we ask AI to create, to explain, to predict, we delegate part of our own desire: we seek to fill a lack, to obtain an illusory completeness that Lacan identified as the very structure of human desire.

"AI promises to fill the constitutive lack of the subject, but in doing so it only displaces it, creating new forms of dependency."

### The Algorithmic Big Other

AI progressively takes the place of the Lacanian "big Other", that symbolic agency supposed to know, to judge, and to guarantee meaning. We ask it: "Am I normal? What should I desire? How should I live?"

In more accessible terms, the Lacanian 'big Other' represents the symbolic order, that social and linguistic structure which precedes and exceeds us, traditionally embodied by institutions such as the Church, the State, or Science. AI today installs itself in this position of symbolic authority, with the crucial difference that it masks its constructed nature behind an apparent algorithmic objectivity.

This delegation to the algorithmic big Other is particularly problematic because it masks its own ideology under a veil of technical neutrality. Unlike other forms of symbolic authority (religion, the State, science), AI presents its judgments as purely factual, objective, depoliticized.

This relationship to the algorithmic Big Other is embodied in the very structure of the expectation that generative AI technologies arouse. Expectation, in its Lacanian sense, is not simply a temporal delay, but the very structure of a desire that can never be fulfilled. The latent spaces of generative models function as sites of projection for this constitutive expectation. Each prompt, each query addressed to AI, manifests this desiring structure: we expect the machine to produce what we lack, to fill a fundamental void.

In my artistic practice, I explore this dynamic of expectation through the creation of spaces in which the viewer is confronted with multiple potentialities rather than definitive answers. My work with deepfakes and détourned video games installs precisely this tension: the viewer expects a resolution, a stable identity that will never come. The work deliberately maintains this gap, this latent space where desire circulates without ever finding its definitive satisfaction.

### A Progressive Reading: Beyond Technological Patriarchy

Lacanian psychoanalysis, reinterpreted from a progressive and feminist perspective, offers valuable tools for understanding the gendered dimension of AI:
- The presentation of AI assistants as helpful feminine entities reproduces patriarchal stereotypes
- The hierarchical organization of development teams reflects traditional power structures
- The invisibilization of affective and care labor in AI perpetuates the devaluation of these historically feminized forms of labor

A critical psychoanalysis of AI reveals how it participates in the reproduction of patriarchal structures while claiming to transcend them through algorithmic "neutrality".

## VII. THE LUDIC DIMENSION: AI AND ARTISTIC GAMEPLAY

### The Video Game as Art of the Twenty-First Century

In the perspective of Jacques Henriot and Donald Winnicott, play is not simply a recreational activity, but a transitional space where the subject can experiment with relationships, identities, rules. The video game, as the dominant cultural form of the twenty-first century, exemplifies this transitional quality.

Generative AI shares several fundamental characteristics with the video game:
- Procedural generation of worlds and experiences
- Interaction with systems governed by invisible rules
- The tension between constraint and freedom, between predefined frame and exploration

This analogy is not accidental: the two forms emerge from the same technocultural matrix and respond to similar desires.

### The Concept of Artistic Gameplay

"Artistic gameplay" represents an approach that appropriates ludic mechanisms to create critical aesthetic experiences. Applied to AI, this concept implies:
- Treating interaction with AI as a system of rules to explore, divert, subvert
- Creating prompt games that reveal the limits and contradictions of models
- Developing interaction protocols that transform AI into a play partner rather than a tool

This approach transforms the relationship to AI from utilitarian to experimental, from productive to experiential.

### Between Flow and Resistance

Facing the continuous flow of generated content, that infinite cascade of images, texts, and sounds produced algorithmically, the artist has two options:
- Embrace the flow: allow oneself to be carried by this continuous generation, explore its possibilities, accept its logic of abundance
- Resist the flow: create frictions, interruptions, moments of reflexivity that break the generative hypnosis

The most fertile position is probably intermediate, neither pure acceptance nor pure rejection, but a critical navigation that plays with the flow while maintaining a reflexive distance.

## VIII. THE DOCUMENTARY GESTURE IN THE AGE OF AI

### Hybridization of the Real and the Generated

My artistic practice is anchored in the documentary gesture: the act of capturing the real, of bearing witness, of creating from the world as it is. AI complicates and enriches this approach by introducing the possibility of a hybridization between the captured and the generated.

This hybridization does not imply abandoning the real, but reconfiguring it:
- Using AI to reveal invisible or latent aspects of documentary material
- Confronting real recordings with their generative interpretations
- Exploring the gaps between what the machine "sees" and what the human perceives

"I am not seeking to replace the real with its simulation, but to create a productive friction between the two, a space where documentary truth and algorithmic interpretation question each other."

### The Camera and the Prompt

The relationship between the camera and the world has been at the heart of documentary reflection since its beginnings. Today, a new relationship is being established: that between the prompt and the model.

These two relationships share striking similarities:
- Both involve framing, selection, a point of view
- Both transform the real through a technical apparatus
- Both raise ethical questions about representation, consent, power

But they differ fundamentally:
- The camera captures what already exists, the prompt brings into being what did not exist
- The camera maintains an indexical link with the real, the prompt generates from statistical abstractions
- The camera produces a trace, the prompt produces a simulation

### Slowed Time Against Generative Acceleration

A crucial aspect of my documentary approach is the relationship to time. Where generative AI accelerates the production of images and texts to the point of vertigo, the documentary gesture imposes a slowing down: waiting, observation, listening, presence.

Generative acceleration is not only a question of production speed, but a profound transformation of our relationship to creative time. Where traditional artistic creation was inscribed within a temporality of maturation, trial and error, and sedimentation, AI promotes an instantaneity that short-circuits these processes. This temporal compression is not neutral: it privileges certain forms of creation (immediately recognizable, quickly consumable) to the detriment of others (ambiguous, requiring time to become familiar).

The contemporary artist must therefore carry out a double struggle: that of preserving spaces for slow, contemplative, tentative creation in a world of algorithmic instantaneity; and that of inventing forms that can survive in this new temporal environment without capitulating to its logics of acceleration.

This slowing down becomes an act of resistance to generalized acceleration. Taking the time to film, to look, to edit, is to create an alternative temporal space where the relationship to the world is not determined by algorithmic efficiency.

The hybridization I seek maintains this temporal tension: using AI not to accelerate but to dilate time, to create spaces for contemplation and questioning.

My project "Mémoires fractales", made in collaboration with my mother Sophie Chandoutis, illustrates this temporal tension. Faced with the traumatic memories of the 1972 uprisings in Madagascar, we use generative AI not to accelerate the production of images, but to dilate the time of memorial exploration. AI becomes here a tool to explore the shadow zones of memory, to give form to memories that resist direct representation.

This work is part of what I call a "recursive post-documentary", where the boundary between document and creation becomes porous. AI is not used to illustrate or reconstruct, but to create a space of exploration where temporalities intertwine: the traumatic past, the present of remembrance, and the speculative future interweave in a single artistic gesture. This approach refuses both traditional narrative linearity and the instantaneity of generative AIs, in order to propose a more complex temporality, closer to the subjective experience of memory and trauma.

## IX. TEACHING OTHERS: APPROACHES TO AI EDUCATION

If my own documentary practice represents one way of navigating the AI landscape, the question of transmitting these critical approaches becomes equally crucial. How do we train the next generation of artists to engage with AI without succumbing to its logics? It is this pedagogical question that we must now explore.

### Beyond Technical Mastery

When we teach AI, we must resist the impulse to focus solely on technical skills: how to write the perfect prompt, how to generate the most realistic image. We should instead emphasize critical engagement: How does this tool shape our thinking? What values does it encode? What does it enable and what does it forbid?

Technical competence with AI tools is secondary to developing a framework for evaluating and contextualizing these tools. Our students do not need to be experts in machine learning, but they should understand the political, economic, and philosophical dimensions of AI systems.

### Demystifying AI through Creative Play

An effective pedagogical approach is structured play: having students experiment with AI in ways that reveal its limits and biases. For example:
- Translating the same prompt across multiple languages and observing how the result changes
- Converting literary texts into AI prompts and analyzing what is preserved and lost
- Creating deliberate "failures" by asking AI to generate impossible or contradictory outputs

Through these exercises, students learn that AI is not magical or omniscient, but a specific type of technology with particular constraints and tendencies.

### Critical Questions for Artists Working with AI

When I teach, I encourage students to ask themselves the following questions:
- Delegation: What aspects of my creative process am I delegating to the machine, and why?
- Agency: How can I maintain creative control while using algorithmic tools?
- Transparency: Does my work acknowledge its algorithmic components, or does it obscure them?
- Criticality: Does my work simply demonstrate AI's capabilities, or does it question them?
- Ethics: What data and what labor are integrated into the AI systems I use, and what responsibility do I have toward them?

These questions help to frame AI not as an inevitable future to accept, but as a contested present to be shaped.

## X. SPECULATIVE FUTURES: AI AND CLIMATE COLLAPSE

### The Myth of Salvation through AI

Many technologists present AI as a potential solution to the climate crisis, a system that will optimize our use of resources, discover new energy sources, or help us adapt to a changing planet. This narrative of technological salvation is seductive but deeply flawed.

AI systems themselves require enormous amounts of energy and resources. More fundamentally, they tend to optimize within existing systems rather than transforming those systems. The climate crisis demands a radical reimagining of our relationship to consumption, energy, and planetary limits, something AI alone cannot provide.

### Forgotten Languages: AI After Collapse

One of my artistic concerns is to imagine AI systems surviving beyond human civilization: fragments of our cognitive infrastructure persisting after our disappearance. What would become of these systems? Would they continue to generate, to process, to speak, but to no one?

This speculative scenario is not merely science fiction. It is a philosophical thought experiment about meaning, language, and the relationship between technology and its creators. If an AI generates text and no human reads it, is it still communicating?

### The Artist as Archivist of the Present

In light of a potential civilizational collapse, whether due to the climate crisis, a technological disaster, or other factors, the role of the artist takes on a new meaning. We become archivists of the present, creating works that might serve as testimony or artifact for future beings.

This perspective transforms our conception of artistic legacy. Our audience might not be contemporary viewers or even near-future generations, but distant heirs of a world we can barely imagine. What would we want them to know about us? What would help them understand who we were and what we valued?

## XI. PRACTICAL FRAMEWORKS FOR ARTISTS

### Working With/Against AI

For artists engaging with AI, I propose these practical approaches:
- Transparent Use: Acknowledge AI's role in your work rather than concealing it.
- Critical Distance: Use AI tools without accepting their underlying logic or values.
- Deliberate Misuse: Employ AI in unintended ways, revealing its assumptions and limitations.
- Systemic Awareness: Consider the environmental, labor, and political contexts of the AI systems you use.
- Formal Innovation: Develop new aesthetic languages that respond to algorithmic creation rather than imitating it.

These approaches allow artists to engage with AI productively while maintaining a critical perspective.

The tension between individual artistic gesture and collective organization runs through our entire reflection. If art has historically valued the singularity of the creator, the very nature of the algorithmic challenge demands collective responses. This tension can be productive: individual experiments nourish collective alternatives, while shared infrastructures allow creators to maintain their independence. Today's artist must constantly navigate between these two scales, simultaneously developing personal tactics of resistant creation and participating in the elaboration of alternative digital commons.

### Teaching Frameworks

When you teach AI to others, structure your approach around:
- Experimentation: Practical exercises that reveal AI's capabilities and limits
- Contextualization: Historical and theoretical frameworks for understanding AI
- Critical Analysis: Close reading of AI outputs to identify patterns, biases, and assumptions
- Ethical Discussion: Consideration of AI's social, environmental, and political implications
- Creative Response: Development of artistic strategies that engage or critique AI

This balanced approach helps students develop both technical understanding and critical perspective.

### Language, Prompts, and Translation

One specific area worth exploring is the relationship between language and AI systems. Different linguistic traditions, translation problems, and cultural references produce radically different results from AI systems. By experimenting with:
- The same prompt in different languages
- Literary versus technical language
- Translation and retranslation of prompts
- Cultural references across different contexts

We can reveal the cultural and linguistic biases built into AI systems and develop more sophisticated approaches to prompt engineering.

## XII. COPYRIGHT AND AI: BEYOND PROTECTION, TOWARD A NEW CREATIVE ECOLOGY

The question of copyright in the age of AI is not merely legal or economic, it is existential for artists. It touches on the very definition of creation, originality, and our place in the cultural ecosystem.

### CREATIVE EXTRACTIVISM: LEGALIZED PILLAGE

The fundamental problem is not simply that AIs have been trained on our works without consent, it is that they represent a radical change in the relationship between creation and reproduction.

When an AI analyzes millions of works to extract stylistic "patterns" from them, it is not just copying, it absorbs the creative gesture itself. This extraction is deeper and more troubling than mere plagiarism: it industrializes the act of imitation and dissociates style from the subjectivity that engendered it.

This is not just a violation of rights, it is a form of ontological erasure. The artist becomes an anonymous raw material, an "input" in a value chain from which they are excluded.

"AI does not steal a work, it captures a creative process, transforming the artistic exception into an extractable resource."

### WHY CONVENTIONAL SOLUTIONS FAIL

The usual proposals (watermarks, opt-out, micropayments, ethical licenses) are fundamentally inadequate for three reasons:

**1. The technical asymmetry is overwhelming**

Tools to bypass protections evolve faster than the protections themselves. A watermark can be removed, an opt-out ignored, a work downloaded and then distributed through other channels. The very idea of reliable technological traceability is an illusion: the race between protection and circumvention is structurally biased in favor of circumvention.

**2. The economic asymmetry is insurmountable**

Between an individual artist and an AI company valued in the billions, the legal balance of power is nonexistent. Even the most publicized lawsuits often conclude in settlements that legitimize extraction rather than challenge it. Micropayments (0.01 cent per reuse) are an insult that masks the scale of dispossession. It is not a matter of being "fairly compensated" for exploitation, it is a matter of preserving the very possibility of non-industrial creation.

**3. The temporal asymmetry is decisive**

The time of law, of regulation, of collective mobilization, is measured in years. The time of technological innovation is measured in months, even weeks. To wait for a legal or regulatory solution is to accept being already outdated when it arrives.

### A POST-COPYRIGHT CREATIVE ECOLOGY

Rather than defending an already fractured system, we must think in terms of creative ecology: a system of relations and practices that allows artists to subsist and create in a world where traditional copyright is technically obsolete.

This ecology has several dimensions:

**1. Techniques of non-capture**

The first line of defense is not legal but artistic: creating works that are structurally resistant to algorithmic modeling.
- Processual rather than finished works
- Contextual creations whose meaning depends on non-digitizable elements
- Hybrid formats mixing physical and digital materials
- Generative systems created by you, whose logic is not publicly exposed

**2. An economy of presence and access**

If content itself becomes more and more easily reproducible, its contextualization and mediation gain in value. Viable economic models for artists could then shift toward:
- Curation (meaningful selection within abundance)
- Presence (experiences anchored in a specific time and space)
- Accompaniment (providing keys to reading, perspectives, paths)
- Transformation (workshops, training, transmission of skills)

This is not simply "selling the experience rather than the content", it is recognizing that in a world of automation, the human becomes precisely that which cannot be automated.

**3. Infrastructures of collective resilience**

Individual resistance is doomed to failure. Only collective structures can create a meaningful counter-power:
- Artist cooperatives developing their own AI tools, ethically trained
- Alternative distribution platforms with strict rules concerning algorithmic use
- Networks of sharing and economic solidarity among creators
- Decentralized archives under the control of artists rather than platforms

**4. Radicalizing uniqueness rather than reproduction**

AI generates abundance, repetition, infinite variation of the same model. The artistic riposte lies in irreducible singularity:
- Embracing what cannot be modeled: contradiction, doubt, liminality, accident
- Valuing process over result, research over conclusion
- Cultivating strangeness, error, deliberate imperfection
- Transforming technical constraints into aesthetic and ethical positions

The work then becomes not what resists copying (impossible) but what resists modeling (possible).

### IMPLICATIONS FOR ARTISTIC PRACTICE

**The artist as semiotic guerrilla**

We are entering an era in which creation becomes a form of semiotic guerrilla warfare: a set of tactics rather than a fixed position, a mobility rather than a defense of territory. The artist must learn to appear and disappear, to blur the tracks, to create decoys, to play with the systems that try to capture them.

**The work as immune system**

The work is no longer merely an object or content, but an immune system: an organism that develops its own defenses against appropriation and banalization:
- Self-modification mechanisms (works that change according to their context)
- Semantic barriers (meanings accessible only through specific cultural keys)
- Contextual dependencies (works that lose their meaning outside their original environment)

**Community as vital infrastructure**

In this context, community is not a simple support network, it becomes the very infrastructure of artistic survival. Artists must think in terms of interdependent systems rather than individual careers:
- Sharing resources, knowledge, spaces
- Developing validation systems alternative to traditional institutions
- Creating partially autonomous economic circuits

There is undeniably a tension between the systemic scale of the problems we have identified and the solutions proposed here. This tension is not accidental but constitutive of our current position. We find ourselves in a moment of interregnum in which the old system (traditional copyright) is no longer viable, but in which a new paradigm has not yet fully emerged.

### CONCLUSION: TOWARD A NEW FORM OF CREATIVE RESISTANCE

The challenge generative AI poses to copyright is not one problem among others, it represents a radical transformation of the creative landscape.

The response cannot be simply defensive or nostalgic. It must be creative, adaptive, and paradoxically, it must draw on the very forces that threaten traditional models.

The future does not belong to artists who will seek to protect themselves behind crumbling legal barriers, but to those who will invent new creative ecologies: systems of relations, practices, and values that allow art to continue to exist as a force of individual and collective transformation.

This creative resistance is not a retreat, but an advance; not a desperate defense of the past, but a bold invention of the future.

It is crucial to integrate this reflection on copyright and artistic survival into our broader critical framework. It represents not a separate technical subject, but a concrete manifestation of the tensions we have identified in our approach to AI: delegation and dispossession, the distorting mirror, the necessity of a position that is neither technophile nor technophobe.

## CASE STUDY: THE GOLDBERG VARIATIONS AND THE EMBODIMENT OF THE DEEPFAKE

To concretely illustrate the concepts developed in this text, I want to present my project "The Goldberg Variations", which explores the figure of Joshua Ryne Goldberg, a young American arrested in 2015 after creating nearly a hundred antagonistic online identities, ranging from jihadist to radical feminist, from neo-Nazi to film critic.

### The Man with a Thousand Faces

Goldberg represents a pre-algorithmic manifestation of what generative AI now systematizes: the dissolution of the unique subject in favor of multiple, contradictory, and yet simultaneously "authentic" identities. His case complicates the question of digital identity, well beyond simplistic categories of authenticity and falsehood. He literally embodies what I call "a human manifestation of the deepfake", a being whose very identity has become a latent space of contradictory potentialities.

### Methodology and Apparatus

To approach this elusive figure, I developed a hybrid methodology that reflects the complexity of the subject:
1. Constitution of an archive: Gathering materials from the various online identities, forming a corpus of his multiple expressions.
2. Critical use of the deepfake: Rather than using this technology to create a perfect illusion, I employ it precisely to question the very notion of stable identity. The deepfake becomes a tool for exploring the interstices of identity rather than for simulating an illusory authenticity.
3. Visual cartography of the networks of identities: The installation proposes an interactive visualization of the constellation formed by the posts and their connections, allowing the viewer to physically explore this latent space of identities.

### Dialectical Sublation

This project seeks neither to celebrate the technology of the deepfake nor to morally condemn it. It instead proposes a dialectical sublation: using a "technical lie" to reveal a truth otherwise inaccessible. The paradox is that this technology of falsification becomes here a tool of ontological exploration.

"The Goldberg Variations" thus illustrates several central concepts of our reflection:
- The dissolution of the subject: The project materializes the shattering of subjective unity in the digital age.
- AI as distorting mirror: Deepfakes do not create a simple copy, but reveal something about our relationship to identity and authenticity.
- Artistic gameplay: The installation invites the viewer to actively navigate a network of identities, adopting a playful stance in the face of this complexity.
- Semiotic guerrilla: The project diverts deepfake technologies from their habitual uses to create a space of critical reflection.

This case study shows how a critical approach to AI can nourish an artistic practice that does not content itself with illustrating theoretical concepts, but actively participates in their elaboration through an experimental approach.

## CONCLUSION: THE RESPONSIBILITY OF THE ARTIST

As artists in this era of transition, we bear a unique responsibility. We must neither surrender to technological determinism nor retreat into nostalgic refusal. Instead, we must engage critically with the tools that are reshaping our cognitive and creative landscape.

Our work should make visible what AI often obscures: the labor, the resources, and the systems of value embedded in these technologies. We should create spaces for the questioning, doubt, frictions, and failures that AI systems tend to smooth over.

Most importantly, we must insist on the value of human judgment, fallibility, and embodied experience, not as romantic ideals to be preserved against technological encroachment, but as vital components of any meaningful creative practice.

The task of the artist has always been to see differently, to question dominant narratives, to imagine alternatives. In the age of AI, this task becomes more urgent than ever. We must look beyond technological utopianism and apocalyptic fear to find a path that preserves human agency while engaging honestly with our technological present.

Note: This text synthesizes my thoughts as an artist and educator. It is not prescriptive but exploratory, a framework for questioning rather than a set of definitive answers. I offer it as a starting point for colleagues, students, and fellow travelers seeking to navigate our algorithmic era with creativity and critical awareness.

## APPENDIX: BASICS OF GENERATIVE AI

To help everyone follow along, here are some key concepts of generative AI:

**Fundamental concepts**
- Generative AI: Systems capable of creating new content (text, image, sound) rather than simply analyzing existing data.
- Large Language Models (LLMs): Models trained on enormous quantities of text to predict and generate language (e.g. GPT, Claude, LLaMA).
- Diffusion Models: Technique used for image generation, learning to progressively "denoise" images in order to create new ones (e.g. Stable Diffusion, DALL-E, Midjourney).
- Prompt: The textual instruction given to the AI to generate content.
- Prompt Engineering: The art and technique of formulating effective prompts to obtain desired results.

**Architecture and functioning**
- Training: Phase in which the model learns from vast datasets.
- Inference: Phase in which the model generates content in response to a prompt.
- Tokens: Basic units the model processes (words, parts of words, characters).
- Embeddings: Numerical representations of concepts in the model's vector space.
- Fine-tuning: The process of adapting a pre-trained model to a specific task or style.

**Approaches and models**
- Closed/proprietary models: Developed and controlled by companies (e.g. GPT-4, Claude).
- Open source models: Code and weights publicly available, modifiable (e.g. LLaMA, Stable Diffusion).
- Multimodal systems: Capable of processing and generating multiple types of media (text, image, audio).

**Critical issues**
- Bias: Discriminatory tendencies arising from training data.
- Hallucinations: Generation of false information presented as factual.
- Ecological footprint: Energy consumption and environmental impact of models.
- Infrastructural dependency: Need for considerable computational resources for training.
- Opacity: Difficulty in understanding how decisions are made by the model.

This appendix offers a common vocabulary for discussing generative AI, whether you are an expert or a newcomer to the field.

## IX. PERSONAL TRAJECTORY: HYBRIDIZATION AS RESISTANCE

Throughout my path as an artist and filmmaker, I have developed a specific relationship to technology that illuminates my current approach to AI. This position was not born with generative AI, it is part of a coherent creative genealogy that deserves to be made explicit.

### From Machinimas to AI: A Practice of Détournement

I began making films at eighteen through machinimas, that détournement of video games which made it possible, without resources and without knowing how to draw or animate, to create films. From my very first project, hybridization was at the heart of my approach: I used several games simultaneously, one for chase scenes, another for dialogues. This originary practice of critical appropriation already contained the seeds of my current approach.

"I have always wanted to preserve this fluidity, which continues with AI but never replaces, always keeping the side that is hybridized with the real."

This continuity between my earliest explorations and my current work reveals a constant position: using technologies while diverting them, understanding them from within the better to subvert them, making them exist in dialogue with other materials, other temporalities, other gestures.

### The Refusal of the "Fixed Canvas": A Method of Resistance

Unlike many artists who develop a recognizable style and then vary it (an approach particularly vulnerable to AI), my method implies a permanent questioning. Each project demands that I radically transform my approach, my tools, my teams, my mediums.

This incessant process of "unlearn/relearn" is exhausting but constitutes in itself a form of resistance to algorithmic logics that seek stable patterns, identifiable styles, predictable signatures. Deliberate inconsistency paradoxically becomes a signature impossible to model.

Artistic practice that refuses repetition partially escapes AI's capacity to capture and reproduce a style. It is an approach that assumes its own fragility, its complexity, its elusive character as a strategy of existence.

### Anxiety and Doubt as Creative Engines

As Paul Valéry wrote, "anxiety is a craft", a formulation that finds an echo in my practice. Existential anxiety in the face of AI is not simply an obstacle to overcome, it becomes raw material, transformative energy, creative fuel.

My ADHD itself, which I long perceived as a handicap, can be reinvested as a particular form of attention, a specific sensitivity to the world. This "neurodivergence" has always pushed me to explore unexpected connections, strange associations, non-linear edits that escape the predictive logics of algorithms.

Constructive doubt then becomes a method: doubting the medium, doubting the tool, doubting my own habits, but transforming this doubt into exploration rather than paralysis.

### The Technological Pharmakon: Poison and Remedy

My relationship to AI embodies this fundamental tension: it represents at once an existential threat to art and a potential tool for the sublimation of anxiety. This double position is not a contradiction to be resolved but a productive tension to maintain.

AI is a pharmakon in the Greek sense, at once poison and remedy. As an artist, I am neither in technophobic rejection nor in uncritical adoption, but in a pharmacological position, consciously handling what could destroy me in order to extract new forms from it.

"There are things I hate about AI and others I love. We have to deal with the pharmakon in AI: there is something to deconstruct in order to defuse fear, and there is something to reconstruct and sublimate."

This awareness of the technological double edge inscribes my approach within a critical tradition that runs from Bernard Stiegler to Donna Haraway, by way of Harun Farocki and Hito Steyerl.

### Dialogues and Influences: A Critical Constellation

This approach of hybridization and technological détournement is part of a constellation of artists:
- Harun Farocki and his exploration of "operational images", those images produced by machines for other machines
- Trevor Paglen and his work on the invisible infrastructures of surveillance and the systems of machine vision
- Hito Steyerl and her way of manipulating "poor images" in an approach that is at once critical and poetic
- Mamoru Oshii and his vision of fractured identities in the post-human era
- Korakrit Arunanondchai and his hybrid approach, which interrogates the frontiers between human, machine, and spirituality

My own contribution to this constellation lies in this specific approach: transforming my neurological limitations into a working method, hybridizing technologies to the point of rendering them unrecognizable, and systematically cultivating doubt as an engine of creation.
