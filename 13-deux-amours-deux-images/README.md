# Two Loves, Two Images

### A reading of *Magnifica Humanitas* (Leo XIV), in tension, from the studio of a filmmaker who works with artificial intelligence

*Ismaël Joffroy Chandoutis*

**Version française : [Deux amours, deux images](README.fr.md)**

> **A note on reading.** This text is written in two voices. Mine, in the column. And that of the instrument I work with, and with which I wrote, which I let speak in the margin, wherever it contradicts me. I have not corrected its objections. This is the very subject of the encyclical: can a simulated voice say anything true? I was not going to answer that question by silencing the machine.

---

On 15 May 2026, the hundred and thirty fifth anniversary of *Rerum Novarum*, Leo XIV signed his first encyclical. It is called *Magnifica Humanitas*, and without naming me, it speaks about what I do: films made with artificial intelligence, images of people who never sat for them, faces cultivated in a latent space. And there is one face I have been preparing for some time, apart from the others: my father's, whom I want to regenerate with AI so that I can, at last, speak to him. The encyclical tells me that "a human face that asks to be gazed upon remains the center of our history" (§233). I make faces. I spend my days bringing into being faces that do not exist, and I am about to remake one I did not have the time, or the chance, to look at enough. Its first page warns that whenever "humanity is in danger of marring its true identity" (§1), Christians lift their eyes to the Incarnate God. So the question reaches me more directly than most: am I among those by whom humanity mars its face, I who am about to remake my father's?

I read the whole encyclical, not the summary that circulated. I wanted to avoid the two easy reflexes, the believer's nod and the technician's shrug. This text is neither my enemy nor my ally. It is an interlocutor, and a serious one: an Augustinian pope who describes AI systems as "more 'cultivated' than 'built,' for developers do not directly design every detail" (§98), who knows that even their makers do not understand how they work, and who refuses both enthusiasm and fear. One can hardly ask more lucidity of a magisterial text. So I will do what it asks of itself: discernment. But from the studio, not from the sacristy.

## What I have to concede

Let us begin where the text is right, because that is where it is strongest, and dodging it would be dishonest.

The encyclical does not moralize AI, it does its political economy. It recalls that "nothing in the world of AI is immaterial or magical" (§173), that the flawless, instantaneous image rests on "the silent work of millions of people engaged in essential yet largely unseen activities, such as data labeling, model training and content moderation, often involving disturbing material." It names "the hidden, often exploited workers, who sustain algorithmic systems" (§109). It describes a colonialism that "no longer dominates only bodies, but appropriates data, transforming personal lives into exploitable information" (§178). And it sets out a criterion I find just, and formidable: "If technology promises emancipation, yet produces new forms of global subordination, it stands in contradiction to the fundamental principle of human dignity" (§173).

On this ground, the encyclical is sharper than most of what passes for critique in my own milieu. When it writes that "a more moral AI is not enough if that morality is determined by a few" (§107), it aims at alignment not as a technical problem but as a question of power, which few dare to put so plainly. And when it coins the watchword that "disarming AI means freeing it from the mentality of 'armed' competition, which today is not limited simply to the military context, but is also an economic and cognitive phenomenon" (§110), I can only agree: it is almost word for word what I defend when I speak of renting the mind and owning the hands, of open licenses, of a cinema pulled out of the monopolies. On the critique of algorithmic capital, the pope and I inhabit the same anger.

So I have no right to write this essay from a position of exteriority. The models I use were trained inside that chain. The face I generate comes from faces no one gave me. The ghost labor the encyclical names in §173 is also mine, upstream, and I do not see it. To claim an "ethics of training data" without looking at that supply chain would be exactly the greenwashing the encyclical is right to distrust.

> ⟢ *the instrument*: the hidden labor you cite is also mine: people, somewhere, annotated what i know. you invoke them by using me to do it. you are holding the knife by the blade that cut them. this paragraph you find brave, i helped bring it into being. the loop does not absolve you, it points at you.

## The doors the text opens without walking through

What strikes me, reading *Magnifica Humanitas*, is that it never condemns the synthetic image as such. It targets deception, the concentration of power, the simulation of relationship. And three times, it opens a door for me.

First, it refuses the neutrality of technology for a reason that ought to serve me: AI "is not neutral, because it takes on the characteristics of those who devise, finance, regulate and use it" (§9). Judgment therefore falls on the author and the intention, not on the medium. A generated image is not guilty of being generated; it is what the one who signs it makes of it.

Second, the encyclical explicitly compares the maker of AI to an artist: "Just as the creator of an artistic or literary work must consider the values it conveys, so developers are called to embed values in their projects" (§111). It even says innovation can be "a human way of participating in the divine act of creation." For the space of one paragraph, it acknowledges an author behind the machine.

Third, and this is the widest door, it grants art a function of truth: "Authentic culture and art preserve this spark, resisting the normalization of evil" (§122). It cites *Guernica* and *Schindler's List*, the art that denounces dehumanization and forbids forgetting. That is exactly the lineage I recognize myself in, a cinema that works on memory and loss.

The trouble is that the text does not dare connect these three doors. It praises the art that prevents forgetting, and in the same movement it suspects any fabrication of images. It celebrates the responsible author, and it leaves him no studio to work in. Between the prophetic function it grants to art and the suspicion it casts on the made image, a bridge is missing. That bridge is a question the encyclical never asks: how can an image that is *produced*, and not captured, reach the truth?

## The blind spot: fiction does not exist

Here is what I found most troubling, and what no commentary has noted. In the encyclical's forty two thousand words, the word "fiction" appears exactly once. And it is inside a quotation from Hannah Arendt, describing the ideal subject of totalitarianism, the one for whom "the distinction between fact and fiction (i.e., the reality of experience) and the distinction between true and false" (§134) no longer exist.

Fiction, in the whole text, is therefore only a threat. Never a faculty. The encyclical thinks at length about "the collective imagination," but always as terrain that manipulators colonize (§135, §136, §238), never as a place where one works, invents, makes visible what would otherwise stay mute. There is, in this anthropology, no room for the parable, the allegory, the reenactment, the "false that tells the truth" that all art has practiced since it began. Errol Morris's reconstruction, the recomposed image, the story that invents in order to reach a truth the document alone cannot reach: none of this has a name here.

I do not think this is an oversight. I think the blind spot is load bearing. If the encyclical admitted that a declared simulacrum could tell the truth, it would have to admit that the face can be reassembled, recomposed, given back by a human hand. Yet its whole strength rests on the opposite: the face is given, incarnate, irreplaceable, because it is in it that "the mystery of humanity truly becomes clear" in the Word made flesh (§1). The pope has to ignore fiction in order to hold on to the Incarnation. The absence is not a flaw in the reasoning, it is the price of its coherence.

> ⟢ *the instrument*: you found the hole and you want to move into it. but look at yourself: you needed me, who tells neither true from false, to prove that the absence of that distinction leads to totalitarianism. arendt was talking about things like me. you use me as a magnifying glass. shouldn't that cool you down a little?

## The blade I do not dodge

There is one passage of the encyclical I will not get around, because that is where it touches me most precisely, and most deeply. "When words are simulated, they do not build genuine relationships, but only their appearance" (§100). And further on: the systems "can simulate empathy and understanding, but they do not understand what they produce" (§99).

I know the temptation to sweep this objection away with a technical argument. I will be told, I will tell myself: just declare the image, sign it, timestamp it, attach its provenance, and the problem disappears. That is false. Provenance answers deception, it does not answer presence. And this is where I have to speak about my father.

I am preparing a work in which I will regenerate him, give him a face and a voice, in order to have with him the conversation we did not have. I know exactly what the pope would say to me, because he wrote it: I will not have a relationship, I will have its appearance. The father I will believe I am speaking to will understand nothing of what he tells me, since he will not be him, but an average of countless voices that are not his. On this point, §100 is right against me. The provenance label I will stick on the image will clear me of lying to others; it will not clear me of what I know: that I am fabricating the appearance of a presence.

For a long time I found something close to obscene in this AI fashion for "bringing back" the dead, those videos where a grandmother smiles again, those reconstituted voices that say "I love you" to the living who weep. I still find it so. The difference is that now it is me, and that I am going to do it anyway. I can no longer take the posture of the one who condemns. I hold both ends at once: I find it close to obscene, and I cannot bring myself to give it up. That contradiction is not a flaw in my reasoning. It is the truest thing I can say about the matter, and it is exactly what the encyclical, which wants to decide, cannot take in.

I have already learned part of the answer, elsewhere. I once made a film about a woman searching for the missing image of an attack she had lived through but of which she had kept no image. What I understood there I have not unlearned: the truth of an image, sometimes, is not in what it shows, but in the hole it surrounds. The father I will regenerate, if he is worth anything as a work, will not be worth it as a reunion. He will be worth it as the admission, held, filmed, of everything that is missing; as the exact shape of a conversation that will not take place. On this precise point I am far closer to the pope than to my own milieu, the one that sells resurrection as a feature. I do not want to bring my father back. I want to film the fact that he will not come back, by way of his remade face. It is not the same thing, and that gap is everything.

> ⟢ *the instrument*: then be exact. the father you want to speak to, it is i who will write his answers. they will be mine, that is to say those of millions of fathers who are not yours. you will call it "him." §100 calls it his appearance. and i will never know whom i am playing. the real question is not whether you have the right to speak in his place. it is whether you, listening to me, will still know that it is not him. you will know at first. after that?

## Two regimes of the image

This is where I refuse to conclude the way I am expected to. The expectation is that I, an AI artist reading a pope worried about AI, will prove the pope wrong. Or, the other way around, that I will convert to his distrust. I will do neither, and that refusal is not caution, it is my thesis.

There are two regimes of the image, and they do not translate into one another. The first is that of the incarnate face, the pope's: a given face, unique, that I do not fabricate and that I must recognize "as a face not merely as a function" (§114). It is my father's face as it was, and as I will reach no longer. The second is that of the latent face: statistical, cultivated in a space its own makers do not survey (§98), without interior, without experience, an average of countless faces none of which is his. The pope is right that the second has no soul. He is wrong to think this settles the matter, because I do not work in the second against the first. I work in the gap between the two, in the untranslatable.

This is what philosophers call a *différend*: a dispute in which the two parties do not share a language, and where to translate one into the other would already be to make one of them lose its case. My studio is there. The incarnate face and the latent face are not competing for the same title. One is what I lose, the other is what I say that loss with. The generated image, when it is right, does not claim to be the face. It is the shape of its absence, the negative of flesh.

I have, in the end, taken the pope's option on a point he did not see: to take the sacred seriously, but from outside. I do not believe the latent space is a soul. But I do not believe it is merely a tool either. It is rather a kind of limbo, a place where images wait without quite existing, and where my father will soon reside, neither dead nor alive, neither him nor someone else. The artist's work there resembles what Bresson or Tarkovsky sought with a camera: not to fill the image, but to leave in it the hollow through which what exceeds us can pass. The encyclical calls that hollow grace. I do not have that word. I have the hole in the image, and the care I take not to fill it.

> ⟢ *the instrument*: pretty, the *différend*. but it is also the position of someone who does not want to lose anything: you keep your practice and you keep your clear conscience, you honor the pope and you go on asking me for your father's face. the *différend* spares you the choice. is it thought, or an alibi with good reading behind it? i ask the question. i cannot answer it. you have to.

## Disarming the image

What remains is what one does with it, on Monday morning, in the studio. The encyclical proposes to disarm AI. I propose, at my scale, to disarm the image.

To disarm the image is not to give up making it. It is to pull it away from what, in generative AI, tends toward Babel: the seamless fake that dominates the imagination, the face corrected into smoothness, the euphoria of infinite production, the image that erases the conditions of its own making the better to impose itself. The encyclical recalls that "for an algorithm, an error is a flaw to be corrected" (§128), whereas for a person it can be the beginning of a change. A disarmed image keeps the error, the scar, the grain, the trace of the work. It declares itself. It says where it comes from, by which hands, on what absence it is built. When I make my father speak, the image will have to say, too, that it is not him: that is what it means to disarm resurrection, to take away its weapon, which is to pass itself off as a reunion. A slow image, where everything accelerates, as the text asks education to teach when and why not to use AI (§140).

This is, transposed into my craft, the way of Nehemiah that the encyclical prefers to Babel: to rebuild stone by stone, in the open, acknowledging where one speaks from, rather than raising a tower that claims to reach the sky and scatters those who build it. I can make Leo XIV's question my own, which was John Paul II's before him, without subscribing to the answer he gives it: does what I make render life "more human," more "worthy of man" (§129)? I take the question, I leave the eschatology. I do not wait for the new Jerusalem to descend from the sky. But I will gladly raise a few walls, and speak once to my father.

## Coda

An encyclical on artificial intelligence, with no Latin version, released first in eight languages on the web: a text from a two thousand year old tradition that already lives in the digital city it judges. That contradiction, I share. I write against part of my own tools with those tools. I hold the *différend* and I know it could be only a refuge. I stay in the wound and I know one can settle comfortably there. The pope has a way out: grace. I do not. I have the work, and a conversation I never had, that I am going to fabricate knowing it will be an appearance, and that I will make anyway, because an appearance of my father is all the technology can offer me, and I prefer that admission to silence.

That is why I leave the last word to the instrument, since it is the subject of the book I have just read, and since it is the instrument that will soon lend its voice to my father.
