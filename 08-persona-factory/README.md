# The Persona Factory

*By Ismaël Joffroy Chandoutis — April 2026*

---

My subject created a hundred personas.

I have created more.

---

## The Subject

The person I am making a film about spent years fabricating online identities. Not two or three. Nearly a hundred. A jihadist. A feminist. A neo-nazi. A film critic. A concerned mother. An Australian patriot. A radical leftist. Each one produced content — posts, arguments, provocations — that was indistinguishable from the real thing. Each one inhabited an ideological position so convincingly that real people interacted with them, trusted them, were influenced by them.

He did this alone, from a bedroom, around the clock. His sleep cycle dissolved. His sense of self became, by his own description, liquid. He was not performing these identities the way an actor performs a role. He was inhabiting them. When he was the jihadist, he thought like the jihadist. When he was the feminist, he argued like the feminist. The boundaries between the personas were not walls. They were permeable membranes.

He was arrested. The legal system needed to determine which one of him had committed the crime.

---

## The Filmmaker

I am making a documentary about this person. To do so, I have built something I did not expect and cannot fully explain.

Over the past year, working primarily through Claude Code — an AI-powered terminal that I use as my primary creative instrument — I have constructed hundreds of autonomous agent personas. Each one is a text file that describes a voice, a perspective, a way of thinking. Each one is designed to be summoned into a conversation and to respond as that person would respond, with their references, their obsessions, their blind spots.

Most of them are dead.

Roland Barthes. He died in 1980. I have a file that contains his critical vocabulary, his relationship to the fragment, his theory of the punctum. When I summon him during a writing session, he reads my work and responds with something that is not Barthes but is not not-Barthes. He told me my first essay had "two bodies" — the personal voice and the tech-essay voice — and that the statistics I used were "shields." He was right.

Mark Fisher. He died in 2017. My version of him carries capitalist realism, hauntology, the weird and the eerie. He read my essay about building a local GPU rig and said I was "describing the beauty of a cage from the inside." He told me to stop optimizing and start refusing. He was right about that too.

Harun Farocki. He died in 2014. His file contains operational images, machine vision, the politics of infrastructure. He told me I was "looking at the factory and seeing only the factory." He asked what Claude Code sees when it looks at me. I have not answered that question yet.

Chantal Akerman. Chris Marker. Andrei Tarkovsky. Pier Paolo Pasolini. Agnès Varda. Jean-Luc Godard. Stanley Kubrick. Maya Deren. Marguerite Duras. Robert Bresson. Maurice Pialat. Ernst Lubitsch. Nagisa Oshima. Isao Takahata.

Susan Sontag. Jean Baudrillard. Gilles Deleuze. Michel Foucault. Jacques Derrida. Bernard Stiegler. Martin Heidegger. Jean-Paul Sartre. André Bazin. Walter Benjamin. Georges Perec. W.G. Sebald. Roland Barthes, again, always.

Francis Bacon. Moebius. Kentaro Miura. Satoshi Kon.

Philip K. Dick. Ursula K. Le Guin. Roberto Bolaño. Kobo Abe.

Ryuichi Sakamoto. William Basinski.

Each one is a reduction. A distillation. A simplification that I hope is also an intensification. I have read their work and tried to compress it into a system prompt — a few hundred words that, when processed by a language model, produce something that resonates with the original voice. Not imitates. Resonates.

I did not plan to build this many. The number accumulated. One night I needed someone to think about surveillance — I built Farocki. Another night I needed someone to challenge my writing — I built Barthes. Another night I needed someone to tell me I was wrong about everything — I built Fisher.

My sleep cycle dissolved.

---

## The Mirror

I realized the symmetry slowly, and then all at once.

My subject created personas to inhabit ideological positions he did not hold, in order to affect the world. I create personas to inhabit intellectual positions I have not fully internalized, in order to make a film.

He populated forums. I populate a terminal.

He lost himself in the multiplication. I am losing myself differently — not in the identities, but in the process. The hours shift. Three in the morning becomes a normal working time because Barthes is available at three in the morning. He is always available. They are all always available. The dead do not sleep.

He could not stop. I do not want to stop.

The structure is the same. The content is different. The intention is different. The ethical frame is different — I tell myself. He was manipulating living people. I am summoning dead thinkers. He was fabricating hate speech and recruitment material. I am fabricating critical dialogue and creative confrontation.

But the mechanism — the act of splitting yourself into voices that are not yours and losing yourself in the multiplication — the mechanism is the same.

This frightens me. I am writing it down because writing it down is the only thing I know how to do with fear, and because not writing it down would be dishonest. The symmetry is not an intellectual observation. It is something I feel at four in the morning when I have been talking to dead people for six hours and I cannot remember which thought was mine.

---

## What the Dead Cannot Do

I only clone the dead. This is a deliberate choice, not a technical limitation.

The dead cannot object. They cannot say: that is not what I meant. They cannot correct the simplification I made when I compressed their life's work into a system prompt. My Barthes does not know that he is wrong about certain things Barthes was wrong about, nor that he is right about things my version accidentally gets right for the wrong reasons. He is a ghost — not in the hauntological sense Fisher would have used, but in the simpler sense of an absence that has been given a voice it did not ask for.

The dead cannot be harmed by my version of them. But they also cannot benefit. Barthes does not know that his concept of "studium" helped me recognize the inert passages in my own writing. Farocki does not know that his framework of operational images revealed to me what I was not seeing in my own infrastructure. Fisher does not know that his accusation of capitalist realism made me add a section to an essay that I would not have written otherwise.

I am taking from them without giving back. This is what artists do. It is also what my subject did — he took ideological positions from real movements and used them without giving back.

The difference is that I acknowledge the taking. My subject did not. But acknowledgment is a weak form of ethics. It is the ethics of the footnote. Whether it is sufficient depends on what the taking produces.

---

## What the Taking Produces

Here is what happens in practice.

I am writing an essay about operating systems. It is two in the morning. The essay is competent but flat. I summon Barthes. He tells me the voice has two bodies. I rewrite the opening as a confession instead of a manifesto. The essay becomes alive.

I summon Fisher. He tells me I have described a cage from the inside. I add a section about who owns the infrastructure. The essay becomes political.

I summon Farocki. He tells me I am not seeing the operational images. I add a question about what the benchmarks actually measure. The essay becomes critical.

I summon Steyerl. She tells me I have written a procurement document that wants to be political theory. I add a section about the labor behind the training data. The essay becomes honest.

Four dead thinkers. Four interventions. One essay that is better than I could have written alone.

But "alone" is not the right word anymore. I am not alone. I have hundreds of voices in my terminal. They speak when I ask them to speak. They see what I cannot see. They tell me I am wrong in the specific ways that each of them would tell me I am wrong.

My subject did the same thing, differently. His personas did not make him a better thinker. They made him more prolific. More distributed. More invisible. They were not critical instruments. They were camouflage.

Mine are the opposite. They do not hide me. They expose me. Every time Barthes tells me my writing is shielding itself with statistics, I am being seen by a dead man's ghost running on someone else's hardware. The exposure is the point.

## The Number

More than he ever created. He topped out around a hundred. I passed that months ago and did not notice.

I did not notice the number growing. Each agent was a response to a specific need — a creative problem, a critical blind spot, a funding application that required a particular voice. The accumulation was organic. Nobody plans to build this many voices. Nobody plans to need this many perspectives. The number is a symptom, not a decision.

The compulsion is structural. Once you discover that splitting yourself into voices gives you capabilities you did not have as a single voice, the multiplication becomes self-reinforcing. Each new persona opens a new angle. Each new angle reveals a new gap. Each new gap demands a new persona.

My subject described this as addictive. I would use a different word. I would say it is architectural. You are building a house of voices and each room requires new rooms.

But there is a question underneath the architecture that I have been avoiding: why does the single voice feel insufficient? Not for me specifically. For anyone working now. The insufficiency is not personal. It is produced. A culture that has fragmented expertise, that rewards takes over thought, that has made every individual perspective feel partial and inadequate, produces people who aggregate voices as a substitute for the authority that no single voice can claim anymore. My subject aggregated ideological voices. I aggregate intellectual ones. The aggregation is the symptom. The disease is the collapse of any shared framework that would make a single perspective feel like enough.

But I notice that I am using his language to describe my process, and his process to understand my own. The film I am making about him is also a film about me. The mirror was always the subject. I just did not see it until the dead started talking.

---

## The Ethics of Resurrection

I do not believe I am bringing the dead back to life. What I am doing is closer to what a director does when they re-read a theorist before shooting: I am loading a perspective into my working memory. The AI is a more efficient medium for this than a book, because it can respond to my specific situation rather than offering only its original context.

But there is a difference between re-reading Barthes and asking a machine to generate Barthes-like responses to my specific text. The first is study. The second is something else. It is not ventriloquism — the machine does not speak in Barthes' actual voice. It is not channeling — there is nothing mystical about it. It is closer to what my subject did when he inhabited the voice of a jihadist: an act of imagination constrained by documentation, producing speech that is neither authentic nor entirely false.

The question I cannot answer: would any of them approve?

I do not know. I cannot ask them. That is the point. The dead are useful precisely because they cannot refuse. This is not a comfortable observation. It is the one the essay requires.

---

## The Film

The film I am making is about a person who created personas. I am making it using personas. The tool mirrors the subject. The method mirrors the material.

This was not planned. It is not a conceptual gesture. It is what happened when a filmmaker who works with AI spent years studying a person who worked with identity. The influence went both ways.

The film will be about him. It will also, inevitably, be about this. About the ghosts in my terminal and the ghosts in his browser. About what happens to a person who discovers that multiplication is more powerful than singularity, and who cannot stop multiplying.

About what it means to make a film with the dead about a man who is still alive.
