# The Missing Chapter

### Reading *Policy on the AI Exponential* (Dario Amodei, June 2026) from the studio of a filmmaker who works with what it describes

*Ismaël Joffroy Chandoutis*

**Version française : [Le chapitre manquant](README.fr.md)**

---

In June 2026, Dario Amodei, CEO of Anthropic, published *Policy on the AI Exponential*. It is, as far as I know, the most politically constructed text ever signed by the head of a frontier lab. Not an abundance manifesto, not an open letter on existential risk: a program. Five policy areas, named institutional mechanisms, cited legal precedents. I read it twice, first as a citizen, then as someone whose daily working tool is built by the author's company. The second reading is the more uncomfortable one, and it is the one I want to write down.

## What the text says

The thesis fits in one image. "The intersection of AI and our political institutions feels a bit like the Hobbits and Treebeard," Amodei writes: urgency facing a creature whose every deliberation takes hours. Technology moves exponentially, institutions linearly, and the danger lies in neither but in the gap between their two speeds. His conclusion: the era of voluntary transparency (2023–2025) is over. "Now the risks are clearly here. It is time to go beyond transparency to more serious and binding regulation."

The program unfolds in five areas:

1. **Regulation and public safety.** An agency modeled on the FAA, mandatory third-party testing above a compute threshold, the power to block a deployment judged too risky, "regulatory markets" where licensed private certifiers do the evaluation work.
2. **Macroeconomics.** Amodei admits what few executives admit: explosive growth can coexist with durable structural unemployment. He proposes robust public statistics, incentives to retain workers, and eventually redistribution: "Mechanisms such as universal basic income could be financed through taxes on relevant companies."
3. **Accelerating the benefits.** Reforming the FDA and EMA to accept AI-generated evidence (predicted toxicology, synthetic control arms) instead of the current seven-to-eight-year approval timelines.
4. **The state and civil liberties.** AI as the ultimate tool of autocracy: a ban on autonomous weapons on domestic soil, closing the data-broker loophole, and one genuinely new legal idea, the right of every citizen to access an AI as capable as the one the state deploys against them, in the lineage of the right to counsel.
5. **Geopolitics.** A coalition of democracies controlling the chip supply chain, sharing it among allies, denying it to adversaries. Because "AI is likely to be the dominant source of military and economic power for any nation," and the gap could be that "of an army of World War II Marines facing an army of medieval swordsmen."

## What must be granted

Three things, before the critique.

First, the candor about his own kind. Amodei writes that companies "have become powerful enough that they capture the state or adopt quasi-state characteristics." An executive naming the risk of state capture by companies like his own remains rare, and Anthropic's governance structure (the Long-Term Benefit Trust) shows he thought about it before writing it.

Second, the right to an equivalent AI against the state. It is the only proposal in the text that invents something legally. If the administration confronts you with a superhuman analytical system, you must be able to defend yourself with a comparable one. It is the Sixth Amendment extended to cognitive asymmetry, and the idea deserves to outlive the text that carries it.

Third, the refusal of both doom and denial. The text holds both ends: the risks are real and dated, the benefits are real and quantifiable, and politics consists in organizing that double movement rather than picking a side.

## What does not hold

**The structural conflict of interest.** Compute-threshold regulation, expensive third-party testing, licensed certifiers: each mechanism, taken alone, is defensible. Together they draw an entry barrier that only established players can clear. Amodei proposes, probably in full sincerity, the regulatory architecture of which his company would be one of the main beneficiaries, and the text does not spend one line on that coincidence. This is not a reason to reject the proposals. It is a reason not to let their beneficiaries draft them alone.

**The moral asymmetry of the coalition.** Autonomous weapons banned on domestic soil, legitimate against the foreign adversary. Surveillance denounced in autocracies, the supply chain weaponized by democracies. The "coalition of democracies" reproduces the bloc logic whose slope nuclear history has already shown: every containment measure is read on the other side as aggression and justifies the very acceleration it claimed to prevent. The text devotes no development to the escalation risk its own strategy generates.

**The unquestioned premise.** The whole edifice rests on the continuation of the exponential, that "country of geniuses in a datacenter" arriving within the decade. If the premise is true, the text is urgent. If it is false, it will have served as justification for an unprecedented concentration of power in a handful of American actors. And the man selling the premise is also selling the company that depends on it. Again, not disqualifying. But a reason to want evaluators of the premise who hold no stake in the race.

## The missing chapter

What stopped me most, though, is not in what the text asserts. It is in what it touches and then drops.

Amodei knows the problem of meaning exists. He writes that the stake, beyond income, is "the need for people to find meaning, purpose, and agency. The latter is ultimately more important." He even offers an argument: "people still devote their lives to playing Chess, or Go, or climbing mountains, and are still revered for these activities, even though all can be done better by machines." And he hopes that "humans can live lives of deep purpose and strive to build awe-inspiring and beautiful things."

Then he moves on. Meaning is named as the most important problem, and it is the only named problem that receives no mechanism. Regulation gets its FAA, unemployment gets its UBI, biomedicine gets its FDA reform, liberties get their safeguards, geopolitics gets its coalition. Meaning gets an analogy with the game of Go.

The analogy does not scale. That a few thousand professional players are still revered after AlphaGo says nothing about what happens when hundreds of millions of people simultaneously lose the structure that organized their days, their status, and their story of themselves. Go survived as a discipline of exception. Amodei's question concerns the general condition. And a transition that succeeds economically but fails existentially does not produce Go players: it produces mass resentment, which is precisely the political fuel that destroys the democracies area five claims to defend. The missing chapter is not a spiritual supplement. It is a condition of possibility for the other five.

## What culture would do, if someone wrote it

So here is the chapter the text does not contain. Not "support the artists" the way one supports a stricken sector, but the recognition of three functions that only culture fulfills inside Amodei's own scenario.

**Making the exponential perceptible.** The text's central problem is one of incompatible speeds, and it is first of all perceptual: nobody *feels* an exponential. Institutions are slow because the humans who compose them live in linear time. Giving sensible form to what escapes direct experience is, quite exactly, the definition of art's work. Harun Farocki did it for operational images, Adam Curtis for systems of power. A cinema that works generative models from the inside, as material rather than tool, is one of the few places where a public can experience what these systems are, rather than read curves about them. This is not a decorative function. In a democratic regime where AI policy will have to be consented to, it is infrastructure.

**Holding the position of exteriority.** Every check the text proposes is internal to the techno-state system: experts auditing experts, certifiers auditing labs. But the counter-power that has historically worked otherwise is narrative: the book, the film, the image that changes what a society agrees to see. The labs produce their own narratives — abundance, doom, the country of geniuses. Artists are nearly the only actors capable of producing narratives about AI *without holding stock in the race*. That exteriority has become a strategic rarity, and it has a material condition: open models, owned tools, a practice that does not depend entirely on the pricing goodwill of those it observes. I say this while working every day with Anthropic's tools: exteriority is not purity, it is a position to be built and defended, including against one's own comfort.

**Prototyping post-employment life.** If Amodei's macroeconomic scenario comes true even partially, hundreds of millions of people will have to reinvent a life that no longer organizes itself around salaried work. One population already lives in that economy: artists. Discontinuous income, non-salaried activity, value decorrelated from productivity, identity built outside employment, days held together only by chosen discipline and chosen community. The artist's condition, precarious and elected, is the prototype of the general condition to come. What artists know about that life — how one holds on, what structures time when nothing imposes it, how community replaces the firm — is an expertise Amodei's transition will require massively and that none of his mechanisms captures.

## The other side

Honesty requires the reverse shot, because the exponential does not only threaten office jobs. Average cultural production — commissioned illustration, catalog music, formatted screenwriting — is automatable before most of the occupations area two intends to protect. The market for functional art collapses first, and none of the text's mechanisms sees it, since for the text culture does not exist as a category.

What will remain, when everything is generatable, is what is not: the situated gesture, authorial responsibility, the fact that a specific human took that risk, looked at that person, signed that image. This is brutal for many who currently live from cultural production, and I do not write it lightly. But it clarifies what art has always been beneath the product that covered it: an address from one person to others, whose value lies in what it stakes.

## In one sentence

*Policy on the AI Exponential* is the text of a sincerely worried man proposing an order in which sincerely worried men like him hold the power: the diagnoses are often right, the remedies almost all consolidate the position of the one prescribing them, and the single problem he himself declares most important — meaning — is the only one he leaves unanswered. That chapter will not be written by a lab. It is, literally, our work.
